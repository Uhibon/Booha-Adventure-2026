The Booha Adventure is our fun, interactive learning world. Students log in with their name and PIN, and the Booha map opens.

The map has weekly checkpoints. When a new week unlocks, the checkpoint glows. Tapping it opens a simple screen with the two decks for that week:

  • Study Deck — vocabulary, sentences, and speaking questions (all with audio)
  • Game Deck — six Booha-only games, including speaking games that accept about 90% pronunciation

Everything is organized by week, so students always know exactly what to use and never get lost in random materials.

年間の計画はありますが、いつも画面に出るのは４週間分だけです（2月第1週から）。毎週月曜日に新しい週がオープンし、前の週は「クリア済み」として残ります。

CEFR（A1〜B2）とは？

CEFR（セファール）は、世界中で使われている「語学レベルのものさし」です。ブーハーリキュラムは、この A1〜B2の範囲を、子どもにも大人にも分かりやすく、少しずつステップアップできるようにデザインされています。

  • A1〜A2：かんたんな単語・よく使う表現・日常の会話
  • B1〜B2：自分の意見・理由・説明・ストーリー、少し長めの文や読解に近い内容

毎週のデッキを使っていくと、「気づいたらA1→A2→B1→B2へ進んでいた」という形で、自然にレベルアップしていく仕組みになっています。

Inside the Booha Adventure, students tap the tree to open the Booha-riculum. This is the yearly learning plan that decides what appears in the weekly decks.

The plan runs all year, but only four weeks are visible at a time (starting from February Week 1). Each Monday, a new week opens, and older weeks become “completed” but stay visible as checkpoints on the map.

What does “CEFR A1–B2” mean?

CEFR is an international scale used to describe language level. The Booha-riculum is intentionally designed to teach the language of levels A1–B2 in a simple, progressive way, mostly through speaking and short reading-style tasks.

  • A1–A2: simple words, basic patterns, everyday conversation, basic writing
  • B1–B2: opinions, explanations, stories, longer sentences, and stronger grammar/vocabulary

The idea is not to “jump levels,” but to slowly and steadily move from A1 → A2 → B1 → B2 by using the weekly decks. Students often don’t notice the level name, but they feel, “Oh, I can say more this year than last year.”

How it connects to Japanese English

The Booha-riculum directly supports:

  • EIKEN 5–2
  • Junior high and high school textbook English
  • The language needed for TOEIC 400–650

Instead of teaching test tricks, the focus is on the real language those tests are built on. That way, when students see EIKEN or TOEIC questions, they’re meeting English they already know how to use in speech.

All students need a device and a notebook for every lesson. These are not optional.

The Booha Adventure and Booha-riculum live online. Without a device, students cannot access the weekly decks, games, or audio. The lesson is built around this system, so “no device today” usually means “no access to the real lesson.”

I also prefer not to spend class time copying words. Instead, students of all ages should write the weekly vocabulary in their notebook at home — English word with the Japanese meaning next to it.

  • Write the weekly vocab at home (English + Japanese meaning)

When students come to class already familiar with the weekly language, the lesson becomes much easier to use. We can spend our time speaking, listening, and thinking in English, not just writing on paper.

少しの家庭学習でも、「前回の自分より一歩前にいる状態」でレッスンに来ることができます。その小さな差が、１年後・２年後には大きな差になります。

1. Students don’t come prepared, so they have nothing to say.

If a child doesn’t preview the weekly cards, they simply don’t know what to talk about. Even confident kids will freeze if nothing is familiar.

A little preview = something to say. No preview = silence.

2. Last-minute practice doesn’t work.

Practicing right before class or in the car feels helpful, but it’s too rushed for the brain to save anything. It’s like trying to stretch after running — the timing is wrong.

To build real confidence, students need small, calm, repeatable practice at home. Even 3 minutes the day before works much better than cramming.

3. Students who never practice stay at zero.

If a child never practices at home, they stay in the same place. They cannot show new progress, so in class we must review the same material again and again.

Not because they’re “bad,” but because the brain cannot grow without tiny, regular practice. This leads to repeating the same answers, repeating the same questions, staying stuck at the same level, and losing confidence because they can’t perform.

The good news: even 3–5 minutes of practice helps students remember faster, speak more, feel proud, move to new content each week, and enjoy class much more.

グッドニュース

大人には完璧な文法も特別な才能も必要ありません。必要なのは少しの週ごとの努力、短い日記、英語への好奇心、 そして「伝え合う姿勢」です。ブーハーリキュラムをきちんと使う大人の生徒さんは、自分で驚くくらい自然に伸びていきます。

The common adult problems (the polite rant)

  1. Speaking English “like it’s Japanese.” English and Japanese are not cousins — they are not even neighbors. Using Japanese logic in English makes everything stiff, vague, and unnatural.
  2. Speaking from a script instead of having a conversation. Many adults want “perfect lines,” not real communication. But English is not a performance — it’s a back-and-forth exchange.
  3. Talking at the teacher instead of with the teacher. English is a conversation, not a monologue. Share ideas. Ask real questions. Don’t just tell me obvious facts about Japan.
  4. Using vague descriptors for everything. In Japanese, “kind,” “easy,” and “good” can cover almost anything. In English, vagueness blocks communication. We need detail: what kind, what part, what reason, what example.
  5. Trying to learn English without engaging with English-language culture. You don’t need to “become Western,” but you do need curiosity. No one becomes fluent in another language while feeling zero curiosity about the people who use it.

Good news

Adults don’t need perfect grammar. Adults don’t need “talent.” Adults need small weekly effort, a diary, real curiosity, and a willingness to talk with someone, not at them.

Adults who follow the Booha-riculum and practice naturally absolutely improve — usually faster than they expected.
